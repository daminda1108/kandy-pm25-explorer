How it works

← Back to explorer

The Kandy PM2.5 Explorer reconstructs a 1 km, hourly fine-particulate field over the Kandy valley for 2019–2026. Kandy's monitoring record is sparse, so the model combines satellite products, reanalysis meteorology, terrain physics and ground measurements from comparable cities — a transfer-validation approach explained below. The satellite-anchored years (2019–2023) carry the headline; 2024–2026 are a labelled extension. This page describes what each part of the map is based on and how much weight it can carry.

The model in one equation

PM(x, y, t) = B(t) + [ T(t) − B(t) ] · Plocal(x, y, t)
  • T(t) is the basin-mean level for each hour. It comes from a gradient-boosted model driven by ERA5 meteorology, GEOS-CF and CAMS chemistry, satellite AOD and NO₂, and solar radiation. Each year it is re-anchored to the satellite-derived annual mean, and its diurnal and seasonal swing is adjusted to match ground observations.
  • B(t) is the regional background that arrives over the area regardless of local activity. It is estimated from a rural satellite floor, shaped by the seasonal cycle and conditioned on air-mass origin.
  • Plocal(x, y, t) is the within-valley spatial pattern (mean 1): a traffic-emission surface modulated by boundary-layer confinement and mass-consistent WindNinja terrain winds (channelling, night-time drainage, day-time mixing).

The basin mean of the field equals T(t) at every hour, so the whole animation can be rebuilt in the browser from a few small arrays. The physics was computed once, offline; nothing is fetched from a server while you scrub.

How the method is validated: proving-ground cities

Kandy has no dense monitoring network, so the fine-scale field shown here cannot be checked against local stations directly. Instead we run the same method, blind, on cities that do have dense networks: we build the map as if the city had almost no ground data, then score it against the monitors that were deliberately withheld from the model. Reconstructing a monitored city the model has never seen is evidence that it is doing something real when applied to Kandy — this is the transfer-validation logic that underpins the whole approach.

The flagship proving ground is Medellín, Colombia — a tropical Andean valley with a complete PM2.5 record and a Kandy-analogue regime. Run blind against its withheld network, the identical engine reproduces the seasonal cycle (r ≈ 0.99), the daily cycle (r ≈ 0.89) and the station-by-station spatial ranking (ρ ≈ 0.78, the best in the panel), with the level landing within about 6%. The same machinery has been tested across a nine-city panel spanning five countries and three continents. Improvements are proven on these monitored cities first, and only the ones that hold up across several of them are ported to Kandy — the wind recalibration and the post-2023 level extension used in this explorer were both validated on Medellín before being brought here.

Extending past the satellite anchor (2024–2026)

The annual level for 2019–2023 is anchored to a satellite product whose current release ends in 2023. A simple chemistry-model scaling proved unreliable for carrying it forward — it misses the emission anomalies of 2021 (pandemic) and 2022 (fuel crisis). So for 2024 onward we replaced the anchor with a model trained on meteorology and a global chemistry forecast: the same driver-anchored approach we validated on Medellín, where — trained only on data through 2023 — it reproduced the 2024 annual level to within a few percent of monitors it had never seen. The 2024–2026 years are shown as a separate, clearly labelled extension tier: the level and timing are modelled, the street-scale pattern is the 2019–2023 climatology, and the level carries a wider (~±15%) uncertainty. The locked 2019–2023 headline is untouched by the extension.

Uncertainty

Every field carries a calibrated 90% interval. The map has an upper-bound toggle, and each numeric readout (basin, core, point queries, health figures) shows its interval next to the central value. The intervals come from conformal calibration on 2019–2023, the same period the explorer serves. Health figures use the GEMM exposure-response function and are always shown with their 95% interval.

Where the map puts the highest values

The readouts distinguish the city centre (the fixed grid cell over central Kandy) from the peak (wherever the highest value sits that hour). They often differ, and the difference is physics rather than noise:

  • Rush hours: emission timing and a shallow morning boundary layer concentrate the local increment near the centre, which then usually holds the peak.
  • Midday: deep convective mixing flattens the field. Differences between centre and basin shrink to a fraction of a µg/m³, well inside the interval, so their ordering carries no information at those hours.
  • Night: terrain drainage (katabatic flow) can carry the accumulated local increment down-valley, so the night-time maximum may sit away from the centre. This displacement is a direct prediction of the wind model, not an artefact.
  • Haze events: the elevated regional background covers the whole basin uniformly by construction; the model never concentrates transported background into the city core. What does concentrate is the locally-generated increment, because the same calm, shallow-boundary-layer conditions that let regional haze linger also trap local emissions. The decomposition panel separates the two parts for any hour.

Documented events

Three documented Kandy haze events are provided as presets: November 2019, December 2022 (island-wide advisories) and February–March 2023 (inter-monsoon). The model reconstructs elevated levels on all three dates through the same mechanism in each case: a high regional background under a calm, shallow boundary layer.

Weather data

The weather panel shows ERA5 reanalysis conditions for the selected hour (temperature, humidity, rain, wind, boundary-layer height). These are the same meteorological inputs the model uses, shown for context; they are historical reanalysis values, not live observations.
